1    Overview
Core Task: Given a video containing dynamic objects (e.g., pedestrians, vehicles), students
are required to design a pipeline to automatically identify and remove these dynamic objects.
Furthermore, the missing regions must be filled using temporal information to restore a clean
background.
Key Concepts:
    • Optical Flow: Temporal Consistency.
    • Segmentation & Object Detection: Mask Extraction.
    • Vision Transformer: Feature Extraction.
    • Generative AI: Video Inpainting.
Group Size: 2 students per group.

5     Implementation Roadmap
5.1    Part 1: The Baseline – “Hand-crafted Approach”
Goal: Implement a complete video object removal and background restoration pipeline using
basic computer vision knowledge and classic models. Understand the underlying logic from
detection to restoration.

1. Mask Extraction: Instance Segmentation
    • Step 1: Semantic Extraction. Use a classic and mature Object Detection/Segmentation
      model (e.g., YOLOv8-Seg or Mask R-CNN [5]) to extract masks for specific dynamic
      classes (e.g., Person, Bicycle).
    • Step 2: Dynamic Judgment. For each detected object mask, calculate the motion
      magnitude of internal feature points using Sparse Optical Flow (e.g., Lucas-Kanade).
      Set a threshold to filter out static objects.
    • Advanced Idea: Apply Dilation to smooth mask edges and ensure coverage of motion
      blur caused by moving objects.

2. Inpainting: Traditional Image Processing
    • Algorithm: Use cv2.inpaint (based on Telea or Navier-Stokes algorithms) to fill the
      masked regions using spatial neighborhood pixel information.
    • Advanced Idea: Temporal Background Propagation. Pure spatial repair often leads
      to blurring. You are encouraged to utilize the temporal redundancy of the video to
      prioritize “borrowing” clean background pixels from previous or subsequent frames at the
      same location. Use spatial inpainting only as a fallback.
Expected Result: Works well for simple videos with static backgrounds but may produce
blurring in complex texture areas.

5.2    Part 2: SOTA Reproduction – “AI-driven Pipeline”
Goal: Engage with the latest open-source academic work and learn how to combine different
SOTA modules to solve complex problems.



                                              3
1. Dynamic Mask Extraction
Requirement: Choose ONE of the following methods to generate high-quality masks for
dynamic objects.
   • Option A: Track Anything [15].
     A flexible pipeline that combines Segment Anything (SAM) and XMem to segment and
     track objects in videos interactively.
   • Option B: SAM 2 [10].
     Meta’s unified foundation model that extends the promptable segmentation capability of
     SAM to the video domain with real-time performance.
   • Option C: VGGT4D [6].
     A training-free approach that mines dynamic cues via Gram Similarity in Vision Trans-
     former attention maps to achieve zero-shot segmentation.
   • Option D: Open Choice.
     Any other open-source method capable of fulfilling the dynamic object segmentation re-
     quirement.

2. Inpainting: ProPainter [17]
   • Reason: A classic work from ICCV 2023. It combines Dual-domain Propagation
     (Optical Flow + Features) and a Sparse Transformer. It effectively utilizes video
     temporal redundancy to fill large occlusions.
   • Alternatives: Students may also choose E2FGVI [9] or FGVC [3].
Expected Result: Handles large occlusions effectively with clear background textures, out-
performing Part 1.

5.3   Part 3: Exploration – “Optimization & Extension”
Goal: Identify limitations in existing methods (e.g., artifacts, inconsistent masks) and attempt
improvements. Choose at least one direction below:
   • Direction A (Mask Upgrade): Introduce SAM 3 [1].
     Idea: Since Track Anything relies on the original SAM, replacing its backbone with
     the state-of-the-art SAM 3 [1] can significantly enhance mask quality. Alternatively, if
     using VGGT4D, use VGGT4D’s outputs as prompts for SAM 3 to refine rough edges
     into pixel-perfect masks.
   • Direction B (Better Mask): Transplant to Stronger Foundation Models.
     Idea: VGGT4D’s limitations may stem from the underlying VGGT model. Transplant
     the VGGT4D [13] method to a newer 3D foundation model, such as Pi3 [14] or MapA-
     nything [7], to obtain more precise masks.
   • Direction C (Generative Inpainting): Introduce Diffusion Models.
     Idea: ProPainter may fail when the background never appears in the video (i.e., pixels
     cannot be “borrowed” via propagation). Try using Stable Diffusion [12] (Inpainting
     ControlNet [16]) to generatively repair keyframes and then propagate the results to
     other frames.

6     Dataset & Evaluation
6.1    Datasets
    1. Wild Video (Mandatory): Each group must film a real-world video (e.g., a corridor
       with people walking) or generate a dynamic object-centric video using a model like SORA
       to verify generalization capability. You can download from Baidu or Google.
    2. Sample Data (Mandatory): The provided bmx-trees and tennis scenes.
    3. DAVIS Dataset (Optional but Recommended): A standard academic dataset pro-
       viding high-quality videos and Ground Truth (GT) masks. Required for high scores.

6.2    Metrics (Mandatory)
    • Mask Quality: IoU mean (JM) and IoU recall (JR) (Reference: VGGT4D paper).
      Evaluate mask accuracy for Part 1 and Part 2 (and Part 3 if improved).
    • Video Quality: PSNR & SSIM (Reference: ProPainter paper). Calculate only for
      experiments with Ground Truth. Note: You must submit the processed video files
      for all Mandatory Datasets (Wild Video, bmx-trees, and tennis).
    • Qualitative Evaluation: Visual comparison of restored frames across different methods.
